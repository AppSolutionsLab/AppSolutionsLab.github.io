U.S. LAW & JUSTICE

Foundational Principles

  1. The Constitution: The supreme law of the United States, establishing the framework for government and fundamental rights.

  2. Separation of Powers: The division of government into three branches: legislative, executive, and judicial.

  3. Checks and Balances: Each branch of government has powers that can limit the actions of the other branches to prevent any one branch from becoming too powerful.

  4. Federalism: The division of power between the federal government and the state governments.

  5. Bill of Rights: The first ten amendments to the Constitution, guaranteeing individual freedoms such as speech, religion, and the press.

 

Legislative Branch

  1. Congress: The legislative body of the federal government, composed of the Senate and the House of Representatives.

  2. Senate: Each state has two senators, serving six-year terms.

  3. House of Representatives: Representation based on state population, with members serving two-year terms.

  4. Passing Laws: Bills must be approved by both houses of Congress and signed by the President.

  5. Enumerated Powers: Specific powers granted to Congress by the Constitution, including taxation, defense, and regulating commerce.

 

Executive Branch

  1. President: The head of state and government, serving a four-year term, with a maximum of two terms.

  2. Executive Orders: Directives issued by the President that have the force of law.

  3. Cabinet: Advisors to the President, including the heads of executive departments.

  4. Commander-in-Chief: The President’s role as leader of the armed forces.

  5. Veto Power: The President can reject bills passed by Congress, though Congress can override a veto with a two-thirds majority.

 

Judicial Branch

  1. Supreme Court: The highest court in the U.S., with nine justices appointed for life.

  2. Judicial Review: The Supreme Court’s authority to declare laws and executive actions unconstitutional.

  3. Federal Courts: Include district courts, courts of appeals, and specialized courts.

  4. State Courts: Each state has its own court system, often including trial courts, appellate courts, and a state supreme court.

  5. Due Process: The legal requirement that the government must respect all legal rights owed to a person, guaranteed by the 5th and 14th Amendments.

 

Criminal Law

  1. Criminal Law: Laws related to crimes and their punishments.

  2. Felonies: Serious crimes usually punishable by more than one year in prison.

  3. Misdemeanors: Less serious crimes usually punishable by less than one year in jail.

  4. Presumption of Innocence: A person is considered innocent until proven guilty.

  5. Right to a Fair Trial: Guaranteed by the 6th Amendment, including the right to a lawyer, a jury, and to confront witnesses.

 

Civil Law

  1. Civil Law: Laws dealing with disputes between individuals or organizations.

  2. Torts: Civil wrongs that cause harm or loss, leading to legal liability.

  3. Contracts: Legally binding agreements between parties.

  4. Property Law: Rules regarding the ownership and use of property.

  5. Family Law: Legal matters involving family relationships, such as marriage, divorce, and child custody.

 

Rights and Liberties

  1. Freedom of Speech: The right to express oneself without government interference, guaranteed by the 1st Amendment.

  2. Freedom of Religion: The right to practice any religion or none at all, guaranteed by the 1st Amendment.

  3. Right to Bear Arms: The right to own and carry weapons, guaranteed by the 2nd Amendment.

  4. Protection from Unreasonable Searches and Seizures: Guaranteed by the 4th Amendment.

  5. Right to Privacy: Though not explicitly stated in the Constitution, derived from various amendments and judicial decisions.

 

Law Enforcement

  1. Police: State and local agencies responsible for maintaining public order and enforcing laws.

  2. Federal Agencies: Including the FBI, DEA, and ATF, which handle federal law enforcement.

  3. Miranda Rights: The requirement to inform arrested individuals of their rights, including the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney.

  4. Probable Cause: Reasonable grounds for making an arrest or conducting a search.

  5. Exclusionary Rule: Evidence obtained illegally cannot be used in court.

 

Judicial Process

  1. Indictment: A formal charge of a serious crime.

  2. Arraignment: A court hearing where the defendant is formally charged and enters a plea.

  3. Bail: Money or property given as security for the release of an arrested person, ensuring they appear at trial.

  4. Plea Bargaining: Negotiating a guilty plea in exchange for a lesser charge or sentence.

  5. Trial: A formal examination of evidence in court to determine guilt or innocence.

 

Sentencing and Punishment

  1. Sentencing: The judicial determination of a punishment for a convicted individual.

  2. Incarceration: Imprisonment as a form of punishment.

  3. Probation: A period of supervised release as an alternative to imprisonment.

  4. Parole: Early release from prison under certain conditions.

  5. Death Penalty: Capital punishment for the most serious crimes, though its use varies by state.

 

Juvenile Justice

  1. Juvenile Courts: Special courts handling cases involving minors.

  2. Delinquency: Minor’s behavior that would be considered criminal if committed by an adult.

  3. Status Offenses: Acts that are only considered offenses because of the age of the offender, like truancy.

  4. Rehabilitation: Focus on rehabilitating rather than punishing juvenile offenders.

  5. Detention Centers: Facilities for housing juvenile offenders.

 

Civil Rights

  1. Civil Rights Act of 1964: Landmark legislation outlawing discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin.

  2. Voting Rights Act of 1965: Legislation to eliminate racial discrimination in voting.

  3. Americans with Disabilities Act: Prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities.

  4. Equal Protection Clause: Part of the 14th Amendment, ensuring equal protection under the law.

  5. Affirmative Action: Policies to improve opportunities for historically marginalized groups.

 

Consumer Protection

  1. Consumer Rights: Laws ensuring the rights of consumers, including protection from fraud and unsafe products.

  2. Federal Trade Commission (FTC): Enforces consumer protection laws.

  3. Product Liability: Holding manufacturers accountable for defective products.

  4. Credit Protection: Laws regulating credit reporting and debt collection practices.

  5. Lemon Laws: Protect consumers from defective new vehicles.

 

Employment Law

  1. Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA): Establishes minimum wage, overtime pay, and child labor standards.

  2. Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA): Ensures safe and healthy working conditions.

  3. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC): Enforces laws against workplace discrimination.

  4. Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA): Provides eligible employees with unpaid, job-protected leave for family and medical reasons.

  5. Worker’s Compensation: Provides benefits to employees injured on the job.

 

Immigration Law

  1. Immigration and Nationality Act: Governs immigration to and citizenship in the United States.

  2. Visas: Legal authorization for non-citizens to enter, stay, or work in the U.S.

  3. Deportation: The removal of a non-citizen from the U.S. for violating immigration laws.

  4. Asylum: Protection granted to individuals in the U.S. who have been persecuted or fear persecution.

  5. Naturalization: The process by which a non-citizen becomes a U.S. citizen.

 

Intellectual Property

  1. Copyright: Protects original works of authorship such as books, music, and art.

  2. Patent: Grants inventors exclusive rights to their inventions.

  3. Trademark: Protects symbols, names, and slogans used to identify goods and services.

  4. Trade Secrets: Protects confidential business information from being disclosed or used without permission.

  5. Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA): Addresses copyright issues related to digital media.

 

Environmental Law

  1. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA): Enforces federal environmental laws.

  2. Clean Air Act: Regulates air emissions from stationary and mobile sources.

  3. Clean Water Act: Regulates discharges of pollutants into U.S. waters.

  4. Endangered Species Act: Protects species at risk of extinction.

  5. Superfund: Program to clean up hazardous waste sites.

 

Health Law

  1. Affordable Care Act (ACA): Expands access to health insurance and addresses healthcare costs.

  2. Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA): Protects patient health information.

  3. Medicare: Federal health insurance for people aged 65 and older.

  4. Medicaid: Health insurance program for low-income individuals and families.

  5. Food and Drug Administration (FDA): Regulates the safety of food, drugs, and medical devices.

 

Cyber Law

  1. Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA): Criminalizes unauthorized access to computer systems.

  2. Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA): Regulates government surveillance of electronic communications.

  3. Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA): Protects the privacy of children under 13 online.

  4. General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR): EU regulation that affects U.S. companies processing EU citizens' data.

  5. Net Neutrality: The principle that internet service providers must treat all data on the internet the same.

 

International Law

  1. Treaties: Formal agreements between countries, which must be ratified by the Senate.

  2. International Court of Justice: The judicial branch of the United Nations, resolving disputes between states.

  3. Extradition: The process of surrendering an individual from one jurisdiction to another for prosecution or punishment.

  4. Diplomatic Immunity: Legal immunity for diplomats from the laws of the host country.

  5. Law of Armed Conflict: Legal regulations governing the conduct of warfare, including the Geneva Conventions.
