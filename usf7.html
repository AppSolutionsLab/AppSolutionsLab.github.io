U.S. GOVERNMENT

Founding and Historical Context

  1. Declaration of Independence: Adopted on July 4, 1776, declaring the 13 American colonies independent from British rule.

  2. Articles of Confederation: The first constitution of the United States, in effect from 1781 to 1789.

  3. Constitutional Convention: Held in 1787 in Philadelphia, leading to the drafting of the U.S. Constitution.

  4. U.S. Constitution: Ratified in 1788, it serves as the supreme law of the land, outlining the structure of the federal government.

  5. Bill of Rights: The first ten amendments to the Constitution, guaranteeing fundamental rights and freedoms.

  6. Federalist Papers: A series of essays written by Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay, advocating for the ratification of the Constitution.

  7. Three-Fifths Compromise: A controversial agreement in the Constitution determining how enslaved individuals would be counted for representation in Congress.

 

Structure of Government

  1. Separation of Powers: The division of government into three branches—executive, legislative, and judicial—to prevent any one branch from becoming too powerful.

  2. Checks and Balances: Each branch of government has the authority to check or limit the powers of the other branches.

  3. Federalism: The division of power between the federal government and state governments.

  4. Bicameral Legislature: The U.S. Congress consists of two chambers: the House of Representatives and the Senate.

  5. The Executive Branch: Headed by the President, responsible for enforcing laws and implementing policies.

  6. The Legislative Branch: Consists of the House of Representatives and the Senate, responsible for making laws.

  7. The Judicial Branch: Headed by the Supreme Court, responsible for interpreting laws and ensuring their constitutionality.

 

Branches of Government

  1. Executive Orders: Directives issued by the President to manage the operations of the federal government.

  2. Cabinet: Advises the President on various matters and consists of the heads of executive departments.

  3. Congress: The legislative branch, responsible for making laws and overseeing the federal budget.

  4. House of Representatives: One of the two chambers of Congress, with members elected every two years based on population.

  5. Senate: The other chamber of Congress, with two senators from each state serving six-year terms.

  6. Supreme Court: The highest court in the land, responsible for interpreting the Constitution and reviewing the constitutionality of laws.

  7. Federal Courts: Lower courts that handle cases involving federal law, including district courts and appellate courts.

 

Elections and Voting

  1. Presidential Elections: Held every four years on the first Tuesday after the first Monday in November.

  2. Electoral College: The system used to elect the President, where each state is assigned a certain number of electoral votes.

  3. Midterm Elections: Held every two years, including elections for members of Congress and some state and local offices.

  4. Voting Rights Act: Passed in 1965, aimed at overcoming legal barriers preventing African Americans from exercising their right to vote.

  5. Primary Elections: Held to select each party's candidate for the general election.

  6. General Elections: Determine the winners of political offices, including the President, members of Congress, and state and local officials.

 

Political Parties and Ideologies

  1. Democratic Party: One of the two major political parties in the U.S., generally associated with liberalism and progressivism.

  2. Republican Party: The other major political party, generally associated with conservatism and free-market principles.

  3. Third Parties: Political parties other than the Democrats and Republicans, such as the Libertarian Party and Green Party.

  4. Political Ideologies: Range from liberal to conservative, with variations within each party and among individuals.

  5. Two-Party System: The dominance of the Democratic and Republican parties in American politics.

 

Government Institutions and Agencies

  1. Congressional Budget Office (CBO): Provides nonpartisan analysis of budgetary and economic issues for Congress.

  2. Government Accountability Office (GAO): Conducts audits and evaluations of government programs and spending.

  3. Federal Reserve: The central banking system of the United States, responsible for monetary policy.

  4. Department of Defense (DoD): Manages the U.S. military and national security.

  5. Department of State: Responsible for foreign policy and diplomacy.

  6. Department of Homeland Security (DHS): Protects the United States from threats, including terrorism and natural disasters.

 

Civil Rights and Liberties

  1. First Amendment: Protects freedom of speech, religion, press, assembly, and petition.

  2. Second Amendment: Protects the right to bear arms.

  3. Civil Rights Act of 1964: Prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin.

  4. Voting Rights Act of 1965: Prohibits racial discrimination in voting.

  5. Equal Protection Clause: Part of the Fourteenth Amendment, requires equal treatment under the law.

  6. Due Process Clause: Part of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments, protects individuals from arbitrary government actions.

 

Presidential Powers and Responsibilities

  1. Commander-in-Chief: The President is the commander of the U.S. armed forces.

  2. Veto Power: The President can reject bills passed by Congress, although Congress can override a veto with a two-thirds majority.

  3. Executive Orders: The President can issue directives with the force of law, although they are subject to judicial review.

  4. Pardon Power: The President can pardon or commute the sentences of individuals convicted of federal crimes.

  5. Appointment Power: The President nominates federal judges, ambassadors, and members of the Cabinet, subject to Senate confirmation.

  6. State of the Union Address: The President delivers an annual speech to Congress outlining the administration's agenda.

 

Amendments to the Constitution

  1. Amendment Process: Amendments can be proposed by Congress with a two-thirds majority vote or by a constitutional convention called for by two-thirds of state legislatures.

  2. Bill of Rights: The first ten amendments, guaranteeing individual liberties.

  3. Thirteenth Amendment: Abolished slavery in the United States.

  4. Fourteenth Amendment: Guarantees equal protection under the law and due process of law.

  5. Nineteenth Amendment: Granted women the right to vote.

  6. Twenty-Second Amendment: Limits the President to two terms in office.

 

State and Local Government

  1. Governors: Heads of state governments, responsible for implementing state laws and overseeing state agencies.

  2. State Legislatures: Responsible for passing state laws and budgets.

  3. Mayors: Heads of local government, responsible for managing cities and towns.

  4. City Councils: Legislative bodies that pass local ordinances and budgets.

 

Fiscal Policy and Budgeting

  1. Federal Budget: A plan for how the government will spend and collect money in a given fiscal year.

  2. Budget Deficit: Occurs when government spending exceeds revenue in a fiscal year.

  3. National Debt: The total amount of money owed by the federal government.

  4. Taxation: The primary source of revenue for the federal government, used to fund government programs and services.

  5. Social Security: A federal program that provides retirement and disability benefits to eligible individuals.

 

Foreign Policy and International Relations

  1. Diplomacy: The practice of conducting negotiations between nations.

  2. Treaties: Formal agreements between nations, negotiated by the President and ratified by the Senate.

  3. United Nations (UN): An international organization founded in 1945 to promote peace, security, and cooperation among nations.

  4. NATO: The North Atlantic Treaty Organization, a military alliance established in 1949 for mutual defense.

  5. Embassies and Consulates: Diplomatic missions that represent a country's interests abroad.

 

Emergency Powers and National Security

  1. War Powers Resolution: Limits the President's ability to commit U.S. armed forces to military action without congressional approval.

  2. Emergency Declarations: The President can declare a national emergency to address a crisis or threat to the United States.

  3. Department of Homeland Security (DHS): Responsible for protecting the United States from threats, including terrorism and natural disasters.

  4. National Security Agency (NSA): Responsible for collecting and analyzing foreign intelligence and protecting U.S. communications systems.

 

Regulatory Agencies and Oversight

  1. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA): Protects human health and the environment by enforcing regulations.

  2. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC): Regulates the securities industry, including stock exchanges and brokerage firms.

  3. Federal Trade Commission (FTC): Promotes competition and protects consumers from unfair or deceptive business practices.

  4. Food and Drug Administration (FDA): Regulates the safety and efficacy of food, drugs, and medical devices.

  5. Internal Revenue Service (IRS): Administers and enforces tax laws and collects federal taxes.

 

Immigration and Citizenship

  1. Immigration and Nationality Act: The primary law governing immigration to and citizenship in the United States.

  2. Naturalization: The process by which non-citizens become U.S. citizens.

  3. Green Card: A permit allowing a foreign national to live and work permanently in the United States.

  4. Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA): An immigration policy that allows certain undocumented individuals who came to the United States as children to receive a renewable two-year period of deferred action from deportation.

  5. Refugees and Asylum Seekers: Individuals fleeing persecution or violence who seek protection in the United States.

 

Social Welfare and Safety Nets

  1. Social Security: A federal program that provides retirement and disability benefits to eligible individuals.

  2. Medicare: A federal health insurance program for individuals aged 65 and older, and certain younger people with disabilities.

  3. Medicaid: A joint federal-state program that provides health coverage to low-income individuals and families.

  4. Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP): Formerly known as food stamps, provides assistance to low-income individuals and families to purchase food.

  5. Affordable Care Act (ACA): Passed in 2010, aimed at expanding access to health insurance and improving the quality and affordability of healthcare.

 

Social and Economic Policy

  1. Social Security Act: Enacted in 1935, established various social insurance and welfare programs.

  2. Civil Rights Act of 1964: Prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin.

  3. Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA): Prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in all areas of public life.

  4. Equal Pay Act of 1963: Requires employers to pay men and women equally for equal work.

  5. Minimum Wage: The lowest wage that employers are legally allowed to pay workers.

  6. Unemployment Insurance: Provides temporary financial assistance to individuals who have lost their jobs.

 

Education and Public Services

  1. Department of Education: Responsible for overseeing federal education programs and enforcing federal education laws.

  2. Public Schools: Education provided by government-funded institutions, typically free of charge.

  3. Higher Education: Colleges and universities that offer undergraduate and graduate degrees.

  4. National Institutes of Health (NIH): Conducts medical research and funds research institutions across the United States.

  5. National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA): Responsible for the nation's civilian space program and for aeronautics and aerospace research.
